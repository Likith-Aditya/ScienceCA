Chapter I

Democritus


Democritus was a Greek Philosopher who lived during 470-380 B.C. He had interests in metaphysics,mathematics and astronomy. He came up with the idea of the ‘atom’ and named it ‘Atomos’ in 442 BC. He didn’t give any scientific proof to his idea of atom, the indivisible particle, but he is known worldwide for is discovery. Fun Fact: He is nicknamed as “The Laughing Philosopher”.

Chapter II

Dalton’s Quest


Dalton was a scientist from the 19th century. It was in this century that the industrial revolution was taking place and people began to question existing scientific theories and try to explore for new ones. It was the year 1803, Dalton was pondering about atoms and the law of conservation. Then he got an idea in his mind. He thought of atoms as indivisible, solid and movable balls. He proposed that they combined in whole number ratios. He also proposed that all atoms of a single element are same in composition. This was enough for his questions but later it was proved wrong by J.J. Thomson.

Chapter III

JJ Thomson’s Thoughts


But Dalton’s model still was not enough. Soon JJ Thomson performed an experiment the Cathode Ray Experiment. (No space to explain but this led to discovery of electrons) This experiment found the electrons. Dalton’s model suggested that atoms was the smallest thing but electrons were 2000 times smaller. This proved Dalton wrong. Now Thomson set out to find a new atomic model. This model was known as the Plum-Pudding Model. Electrons set in some pudding like positive mass which was massless.

Chapter IV

Rutherford’s Ideas


JJ Thomson’s ideas were good for the time but many scientists still did not believe what he said. Then Rutherford who had previously worked with Thomson had decided to try a new experiment. So, around the time of 1908-1913, Rutherford performed the famous Alpha scattering experiment where some alpha particles (From a radioactive source in a lead box) were made to pass through a thin gold foil and were observed on a screen. Most of them did what Thomson’s model suggested, they passed through but some retraced their steps or were deflected. This was observed every time the experiment was repeated. This led Rutherford to believe that the atoms were mostly empty space as most particles passed through but he also said that since some of these atoms were deflected more than 90 degrees through there must be a dense central body in the centre which must be positive (Alpha particles are positive). Since Protons were discovered by now, Rutherford proclaimed that the centre of atoms (Or Nucleus) was made up of protons. Later, Chadwick found neutrons in the nucleus and that was how the nucleus was discovered. This model included a nucleus with electrons just sitting around an atom (Will soon be proved wrong…).

Chapter V

Niels Bohr


Niels Bohr was a scientist who came after Rutherford. He added some more points to the Rutherford’s atomic model as it wasn’t widely accepted. He used existing theories like protons and nucleus along with his own neutron discovery to create a model. In his model, Atoms was a mass with empty space and a nucleus in the centre. There were shells around these atoms and they were called orbits. They were denoted as K, L, M, N. This Model is still widely used as it is the simplest and near accurate model we have. He said that electrons were easy to locate but that wasn’t that true after all.

Chapter VI

Quantum Radicals


There was a new theory again but this time it changed particles as we knew it including the atom. This theory was so radical that it took many years to even understand it partially. It is the most accurate yet unknown field of science. In this magical world of tiny particles where nothing is certain and everything has a wave function, The atomic model was fated to change. This happened in 1926 when Schrödinger a great scientist (who would be remembered for his Cat Thought Experiment) proposed a new theory for the atom. He used many Quantum principles of the time including superposition and Heisenberg’s Uncertainty principle to come up with a model. This was different from Niels’ Bohr Model as it defined these atoms as 3D ellipses which was new. All the electrons where just probability waves with a higher probability near the nucleus. This model became a part of the Quantum Physics. This is the most accurate yet mysterious form of the atom and for simplicity’s sake it is usually the Bohr model which is used in schools today (Along with the concept of Sub-shells which Bohr did not explain).
